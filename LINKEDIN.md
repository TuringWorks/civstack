## Version A — main

Running a country is a finite set of jobs. I tried to list all of them — and decide which ones still need a human.

The result is **CivStack**: an open library that breaks a modern economy into **22 operating systems** — governance, energy, water, food, health, finance, defense, education, care — and maps the core jobs inside each.

Then it assigns every job to a team: a human owner, AI agents, and robots. And it draws a hard line around the decisions that must stay human.

It's now **248 ready-to-use agent skills**. Four things became clear building it:

**1. Jobs are durable; roles are not.** "Get safe water to every household" doesn't change. *Who* does it — a utility, an informal vendor, an NGO, an AI agent, a robot — does. Separate the two and the automation debate gets a lot clearer.

**2. Accountability is the design, not a disclaimer.** Every skill names a boundary AI and robots support but never cross: coercive power, rights-impacting decisions, intimate care, democratic legitimacy, high-consequence safety.

**3. Robots are becoming LLMs with bodies.** I modeled them as LLM-brained agents that issue movements as *tool calls*, executed by vision-language-action policies trained in world models, robot gyms, and RLAIF — with a verified safety layer that can overrule the brain.

**4. It has to fit any country, not a rich-world template.** Every skill carries context modifiers for scale, state capacity, income, informality, and political system. Most of the world's work happens outside the org charts these frameworks assume.

The question underneath all of it: when an AI agent or a robot can do the task, who is accountable when it goes wrong?

CivStack is my attempt to answer that, one job at a time. Open-source — fork it, argue with it. Link in the comments.

What belongs on your "stays human" list?
