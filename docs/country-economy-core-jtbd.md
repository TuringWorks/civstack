## Purpose

This note distills the core jobs required to run a full-fledged country, industry base, and modern economy. It is organized around durable societal outcomes rather than today's org charts, then maps those outcomes to role families, job-board-style titles, AI personnel, humanoid robots, and accountability boundaries.

The framework is meant to be **universal and scale-invariant**: the same set of jobs must be done whether the polity is a city-state or a continent-spanning federation, a high-income service economy or a low-income agrarian one, resource-rich or resource-poor, a stable democracy or a fragile or authoritarian state. What changes across contexts is not *which* jobs exist but *who owns them* (state, market, household, community, diaspora, or external donor), *how formally they are organized*, and *how much capacity* is available to perform them. Read the operating systems below as the invariant layer and the [Adapting the Map to Any Nation](#adapting-the-map-to-any-nation-context-modifiers) section as the configuration layer.

## Research Frame

Inputs used:

- Occupational taxonomy: BLS Standard Occupational Classification major groups and O*NET occupation/task structure.
- Industry taxonomy: NAICS sector logic for how economies classify establishments.
- Critical infrastructure lens: the U.S. critical-infrastructure sectors model.
- Labor-market lens: job-board-style role titles found in modern postings, including AI leadership roles already in this workspace.
- Automation lens: AI agents for digital, cognitive, analytical, coordination, design, monitoring, compliance, customer, and software work; humanoid robots for physical tasks in human-built environments.

**Source-bias caveat.** Four of the five inputs above (BLS, O\*NET, NAICS, CISA) are U.S. instruments, and the job-title lens reflects mostly OECD, formal-sector, English-language postings. They are used here as the most fully elaborated taxonomies available, not as a claim that the U.S. labor structure is the template. Three structural features of most of the world's economies are under-represented by these sources and are treated explicitly later in this note: the **informal sector** (roughly 60% of global employment and the majority of jobs in many low- and middle-income countries — ILO), **subsistence and household production** that never enters wage statistics, and **weak or contested state capacity**, where a "function" exists on paper but no institution reliably performs it. International cross-walks (ISCO-08 for occupations, ISIC for industries) are noted in the sources so the map can be re-expressed outside U.S. conventions.

Key assumptions:

- A "job" here means a necessary outcome the society must reliably produce. A "role" is one way to own, coordinate, or execute that job. AI personnel and robots can occupy portions of roles, but legal, moral, and political accountability remains assigned to humans and institutions.
- Outcomes are universal; **ownership, formality, and capacity are local variables**. The same water-and-sanitation job is performed by a municipal utility in one country, by informal water vendors and household wells in another, and by an NGO or UN agency in a conflict zone. The map names the job; the context determines the operating model.

## The Universal JTBD Pattern

Every national/economic job usually has the same lifecycle:

1. Sense reality: gather data, observe conditions, inspect sites, listen to people.
2. Interpret reality: diagnose, forecast, model risk, prioritize.
3. Decide: choose policy, design, action, resource allocation, escalation, or tradeoff.
4. Mobilize: assign labor, budget, materials, rights, permissions, logistics, and schedule.
5. Execute: perform the work in digital or physical space.
6. Verify: test, audit, measure, inspect, certify, and learn.
7. Govern: maintain legitimacy, safety, accountability, continuity, and trust.

AI personnel are strongest in sensing digital signals, interpreting, drafting, coding, planning, simulating, matching, monitoring, summarizing, tutoring, and coordinating. Humanoid robots are strongest where physical work happens inside spaces built for human bodies: warehouses, hospitals, labs, hotels, retail floors, homes, factories, offices, and disaster-response sites. Humans remain essential for legitimacy, empathy, judgment under ambiguity, social trust, constitutional authority, coercive authority, moral accountability, and work requiring rare dexterity or context-heavy field improvisation.

## Work-System Completeness: the orthogonal support axis

The sections below intentionally identify **core JTBD**: the durable outcomes each national operating system must produce. Core work alone does not constitute a viable organization. Every material JTBD must also be checked against five ancillary work families:

| Family | Job around the core job | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Enable** | Supply evidence, knowledge, data, tools, skills, and reusable platforms | research, knowledge curation, workflow/tool enablement |
| **Integrate** | Connect dependencies, handoffs, queues, decisions, and stakeholders | coordination, expediting, meeting synthesis, consultation |
| **Assure** | Independently check quality, safety, legality, risk, integrity, and completeness | review, testing, audit, challenge, red team |
| **Adapt** | Generate alternatives and turn experience into redesigned practice | brainstorming, scenarios, retrospectives, continuous improvement |
| **Sustain** | Maintain administration, workforce capacity, wellbeing, coverage, assets, and memory | scheduling, onboarding, facilities, succession, workload and continuity |

This is a second coordinate system, not another list of sector outcomes. For each core JTBD, identify only the ancillary services actually required and choose their topology deliberately: **embedded, shared service, platform/self-service, federated, or temporary**. Each service must name its core consumer, trigger, deliverable, service level, decision boundary, outcome link, escalation, and retirement rule. Do not instantiate a full support bureaucracy around every row.

The full method, completeness diagnostic, worked examples, and reusable role map are in [`work-system-completeness-map.md`](work-system-completeness-map.md). Deployable ancillary skills are in `skills/_catalogs/enabling-work/`.

## Automation Suitability Legend

- AI-high: digital information work, repeatable decision support, drafting, analytics, monitoring, software, research, planning.
- AI-medium: work that needs human relationship management, domain judgment, legal accountability, field context, or adversarial robustness.
- AI-low: embodied, coercive, intimate, moral, political, or highly unstructured field work.
- Robot-high: repetitive physical work in semi-structured human environments.
- Robot-medium: physical support, transport, inspection, fetching, cleaning, tool handling, hazardous assistance.
- Robot-low: social trust, rare dexterity, delicate care, combat judgment, high-consequence autonomous force, or deep craft.

## 1. Governance, Law, and Public Administration

Mission: create legitimate rules, enforce rights, resolve disputes, administer public programs, and maintain trust in institutions.

Core JTBD:

- When society faces conflicting interests, create lawful rules so people can coordinate without constant violence or bargaining.
- When citizens need services, determine eligibility and deliver benefits so rights and obligations become operational.
- When disputes arise, gather facts and apply law so conflicts are settled with legitimacy.
- When institutions make decisions, preserve records and transparency so power can be reviewed.
- When new technologies or risks emerge, update statutes, standards, and enforcement priorities.

Role families and job-board titles:

- Elected official, legislative aide, policy analyst, chief of staff.
- Public administrator, program manager, benefits specialist, city manager.
- Attorney, paralegal, legal operations manager, contract manager.
- Judge, magistrate, hearing officer, mediator, arbitrator.
- Court clerk, records manager, FOIA officer, administrative law specialist.
- Compliance officer, regulatory affairs manager, ethics officer, inspector general analyst.
- Civic technology product manager, government service designer, public-sector data analyst.

AI personnel:

- Legislative research agent: compares laws, drafts bill language, summarizes testimony.
- Benefits adjudication assistant: checks documents, flags fraud, explains eligibility, prepares case files.
- Legal discovery agent: reviews evidence, timelines, contracts, precedents, and filings.
- Public comment analyzer: clusters citizen comments and extracts concerns.
- Records and transparency agent: indexes documents, redacts sensitive data, prepares responses.

Humanoid robots:

- Courthouse/public-office concierge, document runner, records-room retrieval assistant.
- Facility security support under human supervision.
- Archive handling assistant for digitization and preservation.

Human accountability boundary:

- Lawmaking, judicial rulings, coercive enforcement, deprivation of rights, benefit denial appeals, and constitutional interpretation must remain human-accountable.

## 2. Public Finance, Tax, Treasury, and Procurement

Mission: collect revenue, allocate budgets, buy public goods, manage debt, and protect public money.

Core JTBD:

- When public services need funding, collect taxes and fees fairly so the state can operate.
- When money is limited, prioritize budgets so public value is maximized.
- When agencies need goods or services, procure transparently so corruption and waste are minimized.
- When financial risks emerge, forecast cash flow, debt, pensions, and macroeconomic exposure.
- When public funds are spent, audit and report results so citizens can trust the system.

Role families and titles:

- Tax examiner, revenue agent, tax policy analyst, collections specialist.
- Budget analyst, financial analyst, treasury analyst, grants manager.
- Procurement officer, contract specialist, vendor manager, sourcing analyst.
- Auditor, controller, forensic accountant, inspector general investigator.
- Economist, actuary, fiscal policy advisor, public pension analyst.

AI personnel:

- Tax return review agent, anomaly detection agent, audit sampling agent.
- Budget scenario modeler, grant compliance reviewer, procurement drafting agent.
- Vendor risk analyst, invoice reconciliation agent, fraud detection agent.

Humanoid robots:

- Mailroom, scanning, inventory, warehouse, and records logistics support.
- Physical asset inspection support for public property inventories.

Human accountability boundary:

- Tax enforcement, budget authority, contract awards, debt issuance, and fraud prosecution remain human/institutional decisions.

- When alliances and trade relationships matter, negotiate agreements and preserve channels.

- When conflict occurs, coordinate logistics, medicine, communications, and rules of engagement.

Human accountability boundary:

## 4. Public Safety, Justice Operations, and Emergency Response

Mission: prevent harm, respond to emergencies, maintain order, and recover from acute incidents.

Core JTBD:

- When someone is in danger, receive the signal, dispatch help, and stabilize the situation.
- When crime occurs, investigate, preserve evidence, and support prosecution or restorative processes.
- When fires, floods, earthquakes, pandemics, or industrial accidents occur, coordinate multi-agency response.
- When infrastructure fails, prioritize rescue, shelter, utilities, medicine, and public communication.
- When risk can be reduced, inspect, educate, enforce, and prepare.

Role families and titles:

- 911 dispatcher, emergency manager, incident commander.
- Firefighter, EMT, paramedic, search-and-rescue specialist.
- Police officer, detective, crime analyst, forensic technician.
- Probation officer, corrections officer, victim advocate.
- Safety inspector, fire marshal, disaster recovery specialist.

AI personnel:

- Emergency call triage assistant, dispatch optimizer, incident summarization agent.
- Crime pattern analyst, evidence chain-of-custody assistant, forensic media review agent.
- Disaster scenario planner, public alert drafting agent, resource allocation agent.

Humanoid robots:

- Hazardous entry, fireground supply movement, stretcher support, debris inspection.
- Shelter logistics, food/water distribution, sanitation support.

Human accountability boundary:

- Arrests, force, triage in scarce life-saving situations, sentencing, detention, and incident command remain human-led.

## Practical Role Design Pattern

For every role in the future economy, design the job as a team:

- Human owner: accountable for goals, values, exceptions, relationships, and signoff.
- AI personnel: research, draft, analyze, monitor, simulate, coordinate, document.
- Robot personnel: fetch, carry, inspect, clean, assemble, assist, enter hazardous spaces.
- Control layer: permissions, audit logs, escalation thresholds, incident reporting, evaluation.
- Public trust layer: explainability, appeal, privacy, bias testing, safety certification, labor impact.

## Adapting the Map to Any Nation (Context Modifiers)

The operating systems and JTBD are invariant. How a given nation staffs them depends on a small set of context modifiers. To apply this map to a specific country, set these dials first, then re-read every section through them.

| Modifier | Range | What it changes |
|---|---|---|
| **Scale** | City-state → small nation → large federation | Whether functions are unified or layered across local/regional/national tiers; minimum viable institution count; how much can be centralized vs. delegated |
| **State capacity** | Fragile/contested → developing → high-capacity | Whether a function is performed by formal institutions, markets, households, NGOs/donors, or simply goes unmet; how much accountability infrastructure exists |
| **Income level** | Low → middle → high income | Affordability of automation, share of subsistence vs. wage work, reliance on imports vs. domestic production |
| **Formality** | Mostly informal → mixed → mostly formal | Whether "workers" appear in any registry, whether tax/benefits/labor-standards jobs even have a substrate to act on |
| **Resource & geography** | Landlocked/island, resource-rich/poor, climate-exposed | Which operating systems dominate (e.g., water and resilience in arid or flood-prone states; ports and logistics for trade-dependent ones) |
| **Demographics** | Young/expanding → aging/shrinking | Whether the binding constraint is job creation and education or eldercare and labor scarcity (which reshapes the automation case) |
| **Political system & legitimacy** | Liberal democracy → one-party → fragile/post-conflict | Where the human-accountability boundaries actually bind, who is allowed to hold coercive power accountable, how much trust the "public trust layer" can assume |
| **External dependence** | Sovereign → aid/remittance-dependent → occupied/sanctioned | Whether key functions are owned domestically, by diaspora remittances, by donors and multilaterals, or contested |

Three worked illustrations of the *same* map under different settings:

- **Small, high-income, aging, trade-dependent state.** Binding constraints are labor scarcity and eldercare, not unemployment. Automation is welcomed to fill gaps rather than feared for displacement. Heavy reliance on imports makes logistics, ports, and foreign relations disproportionately important. AI/robot deployment is pulled forward by demographics.
- **Large, low-income, young, mostly-informal economy.** The majority of "jobs" sit outside any registry; the priority is creating formal, dignified livelihoods, not displacing them. Many operating-system functions are delivered by households, informal vendors, faith institutions, and NGOs rather than the state. Mobile-first leapfrogging (payments, telemedicine, agricultural advisory) often beats building OECD-style institutions. Premature automation can foreclose the development ladder that manufacturing once provided.
- **Fragile or post-conflict state.** Several operating systems (security, justice, public finance) are contested or performed by external actors. The first job is re-establishing legitimacy and basic continuity, not optimization. AI and robotics matter mainly for de-mining, logistics, identity/records reconstruction, and corruption-resistant public finance — not knowledge-worker augmentation.

Practical rule: never lift a staffing recommendation from one context into another without re-checking these dials. A reform that is efficiency-improving in a high-capacity state can be destabilizing in a low-capacity one (e.g., automating tax collection where the binding problem is legitimacy and consent, not throughput).

## The Political Economy of Automation

The preceding sections describe what work must be done and how it can be split across humans, agents, and robots. They are deliberately silent on *who wins and who loses* — and that silence is itself a choice the document should not make. Automation does not arrive as a neutral productivity upgrade; it redistributes income, power, and risk, and the distribution is governed by policy and ownership, not by technology alone.

Core questions every operating system must answer:

- **Who captures the gains?** When an agent or robot does work a person used to do, the surplus flows somewhere — to the firm's owners, to consumers via lower prices, to a software/robotics vendor as rent, to the state via taxes, or to remaining workers as higher wages. The default under concentrated ownership of compute, models, and robot fleets is upward concentration. Counter-levers include public options, cooperative and worker ownership, data dividends, robot/automation taxation, competition policy, and open-weight models that prevent single-vendor lock-in.
- **Who bears the transition cost?** Displacement is rarely symmetric. It tends to fall first on mid-skill clerical, routine cognitive, and routine manual roles, and on the regions and demographics concentrated in them. A map that treats labor as fungible "personnel" hides this. Distributional impact analysis, reskilling entitlements, wage insurance, and place-based policy belong in the design of any deployment plan.
- **Who controls the chokepoints?** Advanced AI and humanoid robotics rest on concentrated supply chains: leading-edge semiconductors and lithography, training compute and data centers, energy, rare-earth and battery materials, and a handful of foundation-model and robotics firms. This creates geopolitical dependency — most nations are **automation price-takers**, buying capability from a few exporting blocs. National strategy questions: build, buy, or ally? How much sovereign compute, domestic robotics capacity, and model independence is worth the cost? What is the fallback if access is cut by sanction, export control, or conflict?
- **Who is captured by whom?** As AI mediates benefits adjudication, hiring, credit, policing, and public services, the rule-writers and vendors gain quiet power over outcomes. Regulatory capture, automated bias at scale, and opaque vendor dependence are governance risks, not just technical ones. The accountability boundaries throughout this note are the partial answer; procurement transparency, auditability, and contestability are the rest.
- **What about the social meaning of work?** Work provides income, but also identity, structure, status, and belonging. Even a perfectly redistributed economy that removes most paid work poses questions about purpose and social cohesion that the culture, civic-life, and social-reproduction operating systems must absorb. "Job replaced" is not the same as "need met."

This is not an argument for or against automation; it is a requirement that any serious staffing map carry its distributional consequences alongside its efficiency case. The honest framing offers both: defenders emphasize productivity, scarce-labor relief, safer and less degrading work, and cheaper services; critics emphasize concentration of wealth and power, fragility, surveillance, and loss of worker leverage. A nation's choices about ownership, taxation, and rights determine which story comes true.

## Failure Modes and Systemic Risk

The automation lens in this note is optimistic about capability. A complete map must also be explicit about how an AI- and robot-heavy economy *fails*, because the failure surface is different from — and in some ways larger than — a purely human one.

- **Correlated failure and monoculture.** When thousands of institutions run the same few foundation models, robot stacks, or cloud providers, a single bug, bad update, model regression, or exploited vulnerability can fail everywhere at once. Human workforces are diverse and fail independently; software monocultures fail in lockstep. Mitigation: diversity requirements, vendor/model pluralism, staged rollouts, and the ability to fall back to manual or alternative systems.
- **Cascading dependency.** The operating systems are interdependent — energy depends on water and communications; finance depends on energy and communications; health depends on all of them. Automating the connective tissue (grid balancing, payments, logistics, telecom) raises both efficiency and the blast radius of any one failure. Resilience requires mapped dependencies, islanding, and graceful degradation rather than brittle optimization.
- **Loss of the human fallback (deskilling).** Each function handed to agents or robots erodes the human bench that could take over when automation fails. After a generation, the radiologists, dispatchers, welders, or grid operators who could run the system manually may no longer exist in sufficient numbers. Deliberate retention of human capability — drills, manual modes, and a maintained skill base — is a continuity requirement, not nostalgia.
- **Agent-specific failure modes.** AI agents fail in ways humans do not: confident fabrication, prompt injection and data poisoning, reward hacking and specification gaming, silent drift as the world changes underneath a frozen model, and emergent misbehavior in multi-agent systems. Robots add physical-safety, sensor-spoofing, and degraded-environment failures. The control layer (permissions, audit logs, escalation thresholds, evaluation) is the answer, but only if it is independent of the system it governs.
- **Adversarial and concentration dynamics.** Cheap, capable automation lowers the cost of attack as much as defense — disinformation, fraud, cyber-intrusion, and autonomous-weapon proliferation scale with the technology. Concentration of capability is simultaneously an economic risk (above) and a security risk (a few points of failure or coercion).
- **Speed mismatch.** Automated systems act at machine speed; human oversight, courts, and legitimacy operate at human speed. Flash-crash-style dynamics can appear in any automated domain (markets, grids, content moderation, logistics). Circuit breakers, rate limits, and human-in-the-loop thresholds for high-consequence actions are essential.

Design implication: for every function in the staffing map, ask not only "can this be automated?" but "what is the failure mode, what is the blast radius, and what is the fallback?" Functions in the [Work That Should Stay Human-Led](#work-that-should-stay-human-led) list are there partly because their failure modes are catastrophic and irreversible.

## Transition Dynamics and Sequencing

This document describes an end-state map, but no nation jumps to it. The path from today's economy to a human-AI-robot one is itself a major piece of work, with its own jobs, frictions, and failure modes — and the path differs sharply by the context modifiers above.

- **Adoption is slower than capability.** Technical feasibility is the smallest barrier. Integration into legacy systems, regulatory approval, liability and insurance, union and labor agreements, capital cost, trust, and organizational change routinely add years to a deployment that is technically possible today. Plans should budget for the social and institutional bottleneck, not the model release date.
- **Sequence matters (and is in this note).** The [High-Leverage AI and Robot Deployment Order](#high-leverage-ai-and-robot-deployment-order) is a reasonable default: digital back-office work first, physical and high-consequence work last. But sequence should be set by *value at acceptable risk*, not by what is merely possible, and it should be re-derived per context — a low-income economy may rationally leapfrog straight to mobile payments and AI agricultural advisory while skipping the OECD clerical-automation phase entirely.
- **The reskilling job is real and large.** "Reskill the workforce" is one line in the labor operating system but is among the hardest jobs in the transition. It requires identifying displaced cohorts early, funding income during retraining, building pathways that actually lead to jobs, and accepting that not everyone reskills successfully. Treat workforce transition as a funded program with owners and metrics, not an aspiration.
- **Institutions change slowly; design for partial adoption.** For a long interregnum, every function is part-human, part-agent, part-robot, with messy handoffs. The [Practical Role Design Pattern](#practical-role-design-pattern) (human owner + AI personnel + robot personnel + control + trust layers) is the operating model *for the transition*, not just the destination.
- **Path dependence and lock-in.** Early choices about vendors, standards, data ownership, and which functions to automate first create lock-in that is expensive to reverse. Decisions that look like procurement are often constitutional. Favor reversibility, open standards, and data portability while the technology and its distributional effects are still uncertain.
- **Leapfrogging cuts both ways.** Mobile money, drone delivery, and AI advisory let lower-income economies skip legacy infrastructure — a genuine opportunity. But automation can also remove the labor-intensive manufacturing and services rungs that historically let poor countries climb. Whether AI is a ladder or a ladder-puller is a policy choice about what to build domestically versus import.

A useful transition deliverable for any nation: a sequenced roadmap that, for each operating system, names the current owner, the target human-AI-robot configuration, the binding adoption constraint, the displaced cohort and its transition plan, the reversibility of the change, and the trigger conditions for rollback.
