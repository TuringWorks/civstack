# The Excellence Map

**A multivariate instrument for reading where a nation or organization *is* — and steering it toward where it wants to *be*.**

---

## Why this exists

Business-process management has a well-known trick: you don't just map the *process*, you map the *personality* of the person running it. Triaster's [personality wheel](https://blog.triaster.co.uk/blog/why-map-personality-and-process) plots a person against the **Big Five** traits and their opposites — open/conservative, conscientious/careless, extrovert/introvert, agreeable/disagreeable, stable/neurotic — on a radar chart. You *catch* your current state, notice it isn't the state the situation calls for, and **adjust to move the needle**. Its loop is Capture → Share → Use → Improve, and its key insight is that **you cannot tick both poles of a trait at once** — choosing one is choosing against its opposite.

CivStack maps the *processes* of a civilization — the 23 operating systems, the jobs-to-be-done, the human/AI/robot roles. The **Excellence Map** adds the missing dimension: the **character** of the civilization that runs them. Same idea as the personality wheel, scaled up — it has three parts:

- The **wheel** is the radar — a nation or organization plotted across **thirteen bipolar dimensions**. It answers *who are we, right now?*
- The **destinies** are the overlay — a set of named **destiny archetypes** (target profiles) you can aim at: higher GDP, top of the Happiness Index, a space-faring generation, the north star of civilization, a lighthouse for technology, the nation others rely on, the nation others want to be. They answer *where could we go, and what would it cost?*
- The **gap** between wheel and destiny is not a verdict — it is a **work order**. Every axis-gap routes to the specific CivStack sectors, strategic missions, and roles that move that axis.

Like the personality wheel, **neither pole of any dimension is "better."** A lean, output-maximizing Engine and a buffered, wellbeing-maximizing Sanctuary sit at opposite ends of the same axes — and that is the point. The Excellence Map makes the trade-off **visible and deliberate** instead of accidental.

## The thirteen dimensions

Each dimension is a spectrum from **0** (left pole) to **100** (right pole). The Big Five were distilled by pruning 18,000 trait words down to five roughly-independent factors; these thirteen were selected by the same rubric, adapted for collectives:

1. **Bipolar** — both ends are legitimate; neither is universally superior.
2. **Measurable** — each has real-world proxy indicators a statistics office or strategy team can already estimate.
3. **Actionable** — each maps to concrete CivStack levers, so a gap can be *worked*, not just observed.
4. **Roughly orthogonal** — the axes describe different things, so a wheel has shape rather than collapsing to one number.

A reading is a vector of thirteen numbers. Plotted on a radar, it has a recognizable **shape** — that shape is the civilization's character. *(Ecological Posture was added after grounding the instrument against how nations actually measure progress — see "How this relates to how nations measure progress" below.)*

## The destinies: seven target profiles

A destiny is a **target profile** — the shape the wheel would have if the society were excellent *at that particular thing*. Each is named, defined, given a target vector, a headline metric, and the CivStack missions that carry it. The named destinies below are the ones you asked for; the Map is extensible — any target vector is a destiny.

| Destiny | In one line | Defining axes (target ≈) | Headline metric it moves |
|---|---|---|---|
| **The Engine** | Higher GDP — maximize output | Optimand→output (low), Slack→lean (low), Production Autonomy & Throughput high, Openness high | GDP / GDP-per-capita growth |
| **The Sanctuary** | Top of the Happiness Index — maximize flourishing | Optimand→flourishing (very high), Slack→redundant (high), Legitimacy high | World Happiness rank, life satisfaction |
| **The Voyager** | A space-faring generation | Time Horizon, Frontier, Production Autonomy, Throughput all very high; Talent deep | Launch cadence, off-Earth presence, %GDP space economy |
| **The Lodestar** | The north star of civilization — moral & civic exemplar | Legitimacy very high, Time Horizon high, Optimand high, Openness high | Rule-of-law & governance indices, civic trust, normative influence |
| **The Lighthouse** | A lighthouse for technology progress | Frontier & Value Base very high, Talent deep, Decision Tempo fast | Frontier patents, breakthrough share, standards & tech diffusion |
| **The Keystone** | The nation every other nation relies on | Openness & Slack very high, Throughput & Production Autonomy high, Legitimacy high | Share of global critical supply, chokepoint centrality, reliability |
| **The Exemplar** | The nation every other nation wants to be | Balanced-high on nearly everything; Legitimacy & Optimand highest | Net talent attraction, soft-power index, composite excellence |

## The gap engine: turning a wheel into a work order

Given a current reading **C** and a chosen destiny **T**, the instrument computes:

- **Per-axis gap** `gᵢ = Tᵢ − Cᵢ` — *signed*. The sign says which pole to move toward; the magnitude says how far.
- **Priority order** — axes ranked by `|gᵢ|`. The biggest gaps are where effort buys the most movement.
- **Alignment score** — `100 − mean(|gᵢ|)`. A single number for "how close is our character to this destiny already." High means you mostly need to *hold course*; low means this destiny is a *transformation*, not a tune-up. Because this number re-aggregates the axes, treat it as a *loose summary only* and the per-axis gaps as primary — a good average can hide one severe mismatch (the classic *compensability* trap that undermines single-number composite indices like HDI or the Global Competitiveness Index).
- **Tension flags** — when a destiny pulls an axis hard *against* a different destiny you also value (e.g. The Engine wants Optimand ≈ 25 while The Sanctuary wants ≈ 95), the Map surfaces the trade-off rather than hiding it. Choosing a direction means choosing what you will *not* maximize.

Each high-priority axis then routes to its **levers** — the CivStack column from the dimension table. The output is an ordered deployment list: *to move toward this destiny, the three axes most out of alignment are X, Y, Z; here are the sectors, missions, and roles that move each.*

This is the same move the personality wheel makes — notice the mismatch between your current state and the state the moment requires, then act on the largest mismatch first — applied to a civilization with an explicit catalogue of levers behind every axis.

## The loop (how you run it)

The personality wheel runs Capture → Share → Use → Improve on a personal cadence. The Excellence Map runs a five-beat loop on an institutional cadence, mapped onto CivStack's seven-step lifecycle (sense → interpret → decide → mobilize → execute → verify → govern):

1. **Read** *(sense + interpret)* — estimate the thirteen axes from indicators. Use a panel and outside reviewers; like the personality wheel, self-assessment alone is unreliable, so a colleague — here, an independent statistics office or peer review — should be able to overrule the inward judgement.
2. **Chart** — plot the current wheel. The shape is the conversation-starter.
3. **Choose** *(decide)* — pick a destiny on the Excellence Map (or a blend). Name what you are *not* optimizing.
4. **Route** *(mobilize + execute)* — take the top axis-gaps to their CivStack levers and deploy the sectors, missions, and roles. Stop at the human-accountability boundary every CivStack skill names.
5. **Re-read** *(verify + govern)* — re-estimate on a cadence (annual for the full wheel; quarterly for axes under active work), watch for drift, and update. Patterns emerge over a few cycles, just as personality patterns emerge over a month of wheels.

**Cadence.** Full re-read annually; active-axis re-read quarterly (matching the command-cadence model in `skills/00-framework/SKILL.md`); destiny review when the strategic context shifts.

## Worked example

A mid-size, resource-rich nation reads roughly: `TH 40, Fr 35, Co 60, Op 55, Ta 45, VB 30, Sl 50, Om 50, DT 55, PA 45, Th 80, Lg 50, Ec 30`. It chooses **The Lighthouse**.

The three largest gaps are **Value Base** (30 → 90, +60: shift from extractive to generative — the defining move), **Frontier Posture** (35 → 95, +60), and **Talent Formation** (45 → 90, +45 toward depth). The engine routes these to OS 15 Science, OS 12 Communications & Software, the Frontier-AI Production mission, and the Frontier Talent Formation mission — *and* flags a tension: its Throughput is already high (80), an asset The Lighthouse only needs at 75, so that abundant energy is spare capacity it can redirect toward compute and research rather than build anew. It also sits low on **Ecological Posture** (30, a +30 gap toward regenerative) — a further sizable gap (OS 19, Bioeconomy) the paired outcome panel would underline, since an extractive resource economy chasing a tech frontier risks overshoot. Alignment score is low, so this is a generational transformation, not a tune-up — and the Map says exactly which levers to pull first.

## How this relates to how nations measure progress

Almost everything the UN and national planners track measures **outcomes** — states of the world on absolute scales where one direction is unambiguously better. The UN's Sustainable Development Goals are 17 goals, 169 targets, and 231 indicators of that kind; the Human Development Index blends life expectancy, education, and income (and the inequality-adjusted IHDI corrects for distribution); the OECD Well-being Framework spans 11 dimensions of current well-being plus four resource stocks for the future; Bhutan's Gross National Happiness aggregates 33 indicators across 9 equally-weighted domains and drives real targets in its Five-Year Plan; the World Happiness Report explains the Cantril ladder with six factors; the Worldwide Governance Indicators report six governance dimensions; and Kate Raworth's Doughnut frames a social foundation inside an ecological ceiling of nine planetary boundaries.

The Excellence Map is a **different layer**. Its axes are bipolar *posture* variables — how a society is configured and what it optimizes for — not outcomes. Neither pole is "better"; the right value depends on the destiny chosen. The two layers answer different questions: the outcome indices ask *how are we doing?*, the Excellence Map asks *what kind of society are we, and is that configuration fit for where we want to go?* You need both. The Map is a navigation layer that sits **on top of** an outcome-and-limits dashboard; it does not replace it.

Measured against that established practice, the Map does two things the single-number indices do not. It **does not collapse to one rank** — by keeping the axes separate and reporting per-axis gaps, it sidesteps the *compensability* and non-robust-ranking critiques that dog composite indices like the HDI or the Global Competitiveness Index. And it **connects to interventions**: most indices stop at measurement, whereas the Map's lever-routing works like a lightweight results-based-management / theory-of-change front-end, tying each posture-gap to the CivStack sectors and missions that move it. That matters because national development planning has surged — from roughly 62 countries with a plan in 2006 to about 134 by 2018 — and the documented weak point is precisely the *plan-to-action-and-financing* linkage that lever-routing targets.

The Map is also deliberately light on two things the 2009 Stiglitz–Sen–Fitoussi report told everyone not to omit: **ecological sustainability** and **distribution**. The new Ecological Posture axis (13) closes part of the first gap. Distribution — who actually receives the output — and horizontal social cohesion are left to the outcome panel below, because they are *ends* best measured on absolute scales rather than forced into bipolar postures.

### The paired outcome-and-limits panel

Every destiny should be validated against a small panel of existing measures, so you don't win the posture and lose the people:

- **Human development** — HDI, and the inequality-adjusted IHDI to catch distribution. *Are capabilities and living standards actually rising, and for whom?*
- **Subjective well-being** — the Cantril ladder from the World Happiness Report. *Do people report their own lives going well?*
- **SDG progress** — the country's SDG dashboard. *Are the agreed development outcomes on track?*
- **Ecological limits** — planetary-boundary / footprint-vs-biocapacity overshoot. *Is the journey staying inside the safe operating space?*

The rule: steer posture toward a chosen destiny, but if any panel light is moving the wrong way, the destiny or the route is wrong. **Posture is a means; these are the ends** — and optimizing a posture axis for its own sake (maximizing Decision Tempo, or Production Autonomy, with no destiny and no outcome check) is the Goodhart's-law failure the panel exists to prevent.

## What this is and isn't

It is a **thinking and steering instrument**, not a scoreboard or a ranking. The numbers are deliberately coarse — their job is to make character *visible*, force the trade-offs to be *named*, and connect each axis to the *levers* that move it. As with every CivStack artifact, the destinies are choices a society makes for itself, the human-accountability boundaries stay human, and you are meant to fork the dimensions, re-weight the destinies, and fit it to your context.
