# The Excellence Map 2.0

**A layered instrument for diagnosing a nation, choosing a strategic direction, and routing the resulting work into CivStack.**

---

## Why the model changed

The original Excellence Map put thirteen variables on one bipolar wheel. That made trade-offs visible and connected gaps to work, but it also mixed four different things: public outcomes, institutional capabilities, structural conditions, and strategic choices. Several axes were not genuinely bipolar. Ecological overshoot is not an equally desirable alternative to sustainability; scarce productive capacity is not a policy style; broad education and frontier research can coexist; and prosperity contributes to flourishing rather than sitting opposite it.

Excellence Map 2.0 keeps the strongest idea from the original—**a diagnosis becomes a work order**—and replaces the single wheel with four linked layers:

1. **Outcomes** — are people's lives and the natural base improving?
2. **Capabilities** — can the state and economy reliably produce those outcomes?
3. **Posture** — what strategic choices and trade-offs characterize the chosen route?
4. **Missions** — which CivStack operating systems, jobs, and accountable roles move the priority constraints?

The sequence is: **Outcomes → Capabilities → Posture → Missions → Work orders**.

---

## Layer 1 · National outcomes

Outcomes are directional. A high value should consistently mean a better public result. They are not personality traits and should not be forced into bipolar scales.

| # | Outcome domain | Core question | Example evidence families | Primary CivStack anchors |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| O1 | **Prosperity & productivity** | Is sustainable real income and productive output per person rising? | Real median income, labor productivity, multifactor productivity, economic complexity | OS 09 Manufacturing · OS 15 Science · OS 16 Finance · OS 20 Labor |
| O2 | **Human development** | Are people living longer, healthier, more capable lives? | HDI/IHDI components, healthy life expectancy, learning-adjusted education | OS 13 Healthcare · OS 14 Education · OS 21 Household & Care |
| O3 | **Good work & economic security** | Can people obtain productive work and withstand ordinary economic shocks? | Employment, participation, informality, real wages, underemployment, household buffers | OS 20 Labor · OS 16 Finance · OS 21 Household & Care |
| O4 | **Inclusion & mobility** | Are gains broadly shared across places, classes, genders, and generations? | Income/wealth distribution, poverty, intergenerational mobility, regional and demographic gaps | OS 01 Governance · OS 14 Education · OS 20 Labor · OS 21 |
| O5 | **Safety & lived wellbeing** | Are people secure, free, socially connected, and satisfied with their lives? | Personal safety, life satisfaction, trust, loneliness, housing burden, time use | OS 04 Public Safety · OS 10 Shelter · OS 18 Media & Civic · OS 21 |
| O6 | **Ecological sustainability** | Are human needs met while preserving climate and ecosystem stability? | Territorial and consumption emissions, material footprint, biodiversity, air/water quality, circularity | OS 05 Food · OS 06 Water · OS 07 Energy · OS 19 Environment |

No single “national excellence” score is required. Report the six domains separately, including level, trend, distribution, uncertainty, and peer context.

## Layer 2 · Institutional and productive capabilities

Capabilities are the durable means by which outcomes are produced. They are also directional: stronger capability is generally preferable, even though the institutional form used to create it may differ.

| # | Capability | What is being tested | Example evidence families | Primary CivStack anchors |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| C1 | **Macroeconomic stability** | Are inflation, output, employment, and monetary conditions stable enough for long-horizon action? | Inflation level/volatility, output volatility, monetary credibility | OS 02 Public Finance · OS 16 Finance |
| C2 | **Fiscal capacity & sustainability** | Can the state raise, allocate, and account for resources without destabilizing debt? | Revenue capacity, interest burden, debt trajectory, budget credibility, audit quality | OS 02 Public Finance · OS 01 Governance |
| C3 | **Financial & external resilience** | Can the economy absorb banking, currency, capital-flow, and balance-of-payments shocks? | Bank soundness, reserves, current account, external debt, funding concentration | OS 16 Finance · OS 03 Defense & Foreign |
| C4 | **Government effectiveness** | Can public institutions design, coordinate, and deliver services reliably? | Service quality, implementation record, civil-service capability, procurement performance | OS 01 Governance · human-command roles |
| C5 | **Rule of law & regulatory quality** | Are rules predictable, enforceable, proportionate, and contestable? | Rule of law, contract enforcement, regulatory quality, justice access | OS 01 Governance · OS 23 Identity & DPI |
| C6 | **Accountability & integrity** | Can people scrutinize authority and correct corruption, capture, and administrative abuse? | Voice, transparency, corruption control, audit, grievance and redress | OS 01 · OS 18 Media & Civic · OS 23 Identity & DPI |
| C7 | **Human capital** | Does the country form broad capability and enough deep expertise? | Foundational learning, skills, health, research talent, lifelong learning | OS 13 Healthcare · OS 14 Education · OS 20 Labor |
| C8 | **Infrastructure & digital capacity** | Are energy, water, transport, housing, communications, and public digital rails reliable and accessible? | Coverage, quality, affordability, reliability, interoperability | OS 06 Water · OS 07 Energy · OS 10 Shelter · OS 11 Logistics · OS 12 Communications · OS 23 |
| C9 | **Innovation & productive sophistication** | Can ideas become scalable firms, technologies, exports, and public capability? | R&D, diffusion, firm dynamism, patents, complexity, technology transfer | OS 09 Manufacturing · OS 12 Software · OS 15 Science · frontier missions |
| C10 | **Resilience & security** | Can essential systems continue through disasters, conflict, cyber events, and supply disruption? | Stress tests, reserves, continuity, redundancy, recovery time, defense readiness | OS 03 Defense · OS 04 Emergency · OS 22 Resilience |
| C11 | **International position & reliability** | Can the nation attract, cooperate, influence standards, and honor commitments without dangerous dependence? | Trade and talent attraction, alliance reliability, soft power, supply-chain centrality | OS 03 Defense & Foreign · OS 11 Logistics · OS 16 Finance |

This layer deliberately mirrors the concerns visible in IMF surveillance, World Bank governance diagnostics, sovereign-risk analysis, and OECD/UN development frameworks without reproducing any one institution's ideology or composite score.

These capabilities are not fully orthogonal — C1 (macroeconomic stability) and C3 (financial & external resilience) overlap on financial stability, and C10 (resilience & security) brushes the safety and ecological outcome domains. The redundancy-and-correlation test in the measurement protocol applies to these first; split or merge them in context rather than treating the eleven as independent by assumption.

### Research anchors

- [IMF surveillance](https://www.imf.org/en/about/factsheets/imf-surveillance) — fiscal, monetary, exchange-rate, financial, structural, external, climate, and digital risks.
- [World Bank Worldwide Governance Indicators](https://www.worldbank.org/en/publication/worldwide-governance-indicators) — voice and accountability, political stability, government effectiveness, regulatory quality, rule of law, and control of corruption, with an explicit warning that broad composites are too coarse for reform design.
- [UNDP Human Development Index](https://hdr.undp.org/data-center/human-development-index) — health, education, and income capabilities, including inequality-sensitive variants.
- [United Nations Sustainable Development Goals](https://sdgs.un.org/goals) — directional social, economic, institutional, and ecological outcomes.

These frameworks are evidence families, not a ready-made CivStack ontology. The Map's distinct contribution is the route from diagnosis to accountable implementation.

## Layer 3 · Strategic posture

Posture variables are genuine choices. Neither end is automatically excellent; fitness depends on mission, context, institutional quality, and timing.

| # | Posture choice | Left pole | Right pole | What it clarifies |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| P1 | **Time orientation** | Short-cycle responsiveness | Intergenerational commitment | How far ahead commitments and capital allocation extend |
| P2 | **Coordination mode** | Market / emergent | Mission / directed | How collective effort is discovered and mobilized |
| P3 | **External orientation** | Domestic autonomy | International integration | How exposure, exchange, and strategic dependence are balanced |
| P4 | **Innovation posture** | Consolidation / diffusion | Frontier exploration | Whether effort favors broad adoption or uncertain breakthroughs |
| P5 | **Efficiency posture** | Lean optimization | Buffered redundancy | How much option value and spare capacity are maintained |
| P6 | **Authority pattern** | Distributed / local | Centralized / national | Where decision rights and implementation authority sit |
| P7 | **Decision process** | Deliberative / consensus | Rapid / executive | How participation, evidence, speed, and reversibility are traded |
| P8 | **Talent emphasis** | Broad capability | Specialist concentration | Where marginal education and talent investment is concentrated |
| P9 | **Operating model** | Human-intensive | Machine-intensive | How work is allocated across people, AI, robots, and autonomous systems |
| P10 | **Objective emphasis** | Production & income | Wellbeing & time | Which outcomes receive marginal priority after minimum safeguards hold |

Posture scores describe **location**, not quality. The Map may calculate distance from a chosen destiny's posture, but it must never call one end superior or combine posture with outcome performance into an “excellence” total.

## Layer 4 · Destinies and missions

A destiny is now a structured strategic hypothesis rather than a thirteen-number personality polygon. Each destiny contains:

- **Outcome thresholds** — the public results that must improve or be protected.
- **Capability requirements** — the institutional strengths the strategy depends on.
- **Posture preferences** — the strategic route the archetype tends to favor.
- **Guardrails** — outcomes that may not be sacrificed while pursuing the mission.
- **CivStack routes** — operating systems, JTBD, and accountable roles that can move the constraint.

The seven archetypes remain useful:

| Destiny | Primary ambition | Non-negotiable guardrails |
|---|---|---|
| **The Engine** | Rapid productivity, income, and scalable production | Fiscal/financial stability, inclusion floor, ecological trajectory |
| **The Sanctuary** | Human development, security, time, and lived wellbeing | Sustainable fiscal base, adequate productivity, resilience |
| **The Voyager** | Durable off-Earth scientific and industrial capability | Broad human development, mission legitimacy, terrestrial sustainability |
| **The Lodestar** | Trusted, rights-respecting, future-oriented civic leadership | Government effectiveness, security, prosperity sufficient to sustain commitments |
| **The Lighthouse** | Create and diffuse frontier technologies | Broad capability formation, integrity, safe diffusion, transition support |
| **The Keystone** | Become a reliable node in critical global systems | Domestic resilience, partner trust, diversification, ecological responsibility |
| **The Exemplar** | Achieve strong outcomes across all domains without hidden sacrifice | No outcome domain below its agreed floor |

Destiny targets in the interactive prototype are **illustrative policy hypotheses**, not empirical league-table cutoffs. A real deployment must set thresholds through public mandate, evidence, peer comparison, and fiscal analysis.

## From diagnosis to work order

The routing engine follows a constrained sequence:

1. **Find outcome deficits.** Identify domains below an agreed floor or moving in the wrong direction.
2. **Diagnose binding capabilities.** Determine which institutional or productive constraints causally contribute to those deficits.
3. **Check posture fit.** Ask whether the current strategic posture supports the chosen destiny; do not treat posture distance as failure by itself.
4. **Rank interventions.** Use a declared decision rule such as `expected outcome impact × urgency × tractability × evidence confidence ÷ cost and risk`.
5. **Route into CivStack.** Assign the highest priorities to operating systems, missions, JTBD, and named accountable roles.
6. **Verify and adapt.** Track leading capability indicators and lagging outcomes; stop or redesign work that improves the proxy but harms the outcome.

The interactive tool uses target-weighted capability gaps only as a **routing prompt**. It intentionally does not produce an overall excellence or alignment score.

## Measurement protocol

Every production indicator must carry:

- definition, unit, direction, source, owner, geography, population coverage, and reference period;
- raw value, normalized value, trend, peer percentile, and uncertainty or confidence;
- disaggregation by relevant region and population group;
- transformation and weighting rules, including missing-data treatment;
- a link from capability to outcome that states the causal hypothesis and contrary evidence;
- revision history so changes in data or methodology do not masquerade as national progress.

Use fixed, interpretable thresholds where possible. Use peer percentiles only for context, not as moral truth. Never encode “unknown” as 50. Do not average domains unless decision-makers explicitly publish the weights and sensitivity analysis shows that the conclusion is robust.

### Validation before institutional use

1. Back-test indicators across countries and time.
2. Test redundancy and correlation; split domains that hide distinct constructs.
3. Run expert and public review, including groups affected by the resulting interventions.
4. Test inter-rater reliability for qualitative evidence.
5. Publish sensitivity analysis for normalization, thresholds, and weights.
6. Pilot the routing engine on several contrasting countries before using it for resource allocation.

---

## Entity scope

This version is a **national assessment model**. Organizations and coalitions can use the same four-layer logic, but not the same indicators. An organizational profile should replace sovereign macroeconomics, public legitimacy, and national security with financial sustainability, governance, operational reliability, workforce capability, stakeholder trust, ecosystem position, and mission impact. Do not compare a company, city, ministry, and country on one nominal scale.

---

## Operating cadence

- **Outcomes:** annual or when authoritative data refreshes; key safety indicators more frequently.
- **Capabilities:** quarterly for active constraints, annually for the complete review.
- **Posture:** review when strategy, leadership mandate, or external conditions materially change.
- **Missions:** monthly delivery review and quarterly outcome/capability review.
- **Methodology:** independent review at least every two years.

The governing rule is simple: **posture is a choice, capability is a means, outcomes are the test, and missions are the work.**
