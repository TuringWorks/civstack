# Excellence Map 2.0 — Domain and Mission Levers

**How outcome deficits and capability constraints become accountable CivStack work.**

This companion to [`excellence-map.md`](excellence-map.md) replaces the original thirteen-axis lever catalogue. Outcomes, capabilities, and posture choices now play different roles:

- an **outcome deficit** establishes why action matters;
- a **capability constraint** identifies what prevents improvement;
- a **posture choice** describes the route and its trade-offs;
- a **mission work order** names what to build, who owns it, and how success will be verified.

All automated work inherits the human-accountability boundaries of the relevant CivStack skill. Policy authority, coercive decisions, rights restrictions, public-resource allocation, and acceptance of residual risk remain human-accountable.

## Posture → design implications

Posture is not “improved” toward the right-hand pole. It changes the design of the work order.

| Posture choice | If the route leans left | If the route leans right |
|---|---|---|
| Short-cycle ↔ intergenerational | Use reversible pilots and rapid feedback | Protect long-lived commitments and intergenerational tests |
| Market ↔ mission coordination | Build discovery, competition, and price signals | Set mission governance, milestones, procurement, and sunset rules |
| Domestic autonomy ↔ integration | Diversify domestic capacity and critical reserves | Build interoperability, alliances, and dependency transparency |
| Consolidation ↔ frontier exploration | Prioritize diffusion, reliability, and adoption | Run option portfolios, sandboxes, and cheap governed failure |
| Lean ↔ buffered | Remove queues and duplication | Fund reserves, alternate suppliers, fallback, and exercises |
| Distributed ↔ centralized authority | Standardize interfaces and local accountability | Clarify national command, escalation, and checks on concentration |
| Deliberative ↔ rapid decision | Fund participation, evidence review, and consent | Require decision logs, reversibility, appeal, and after-action review |
| Broad ↔ specialist talent | Scale foundational and vocational capability | Build deep pipelines, research teams, and global recruitment |
| Human ↔ machine intensive | Strengthen staffing, craft, and service relationships | Stage automation, assurance, transition support, and human keep-warm |
| Production ↔ wellbeing emphasis | Protect minimum social and ecological floors | Protect fiscal/productive foundations and long-run option value |

## Work-order template

Every routed intervention should contain:

1. **Outcome:** which public result must change, for whom, by how much, and by when?
2. **Constraint:** what evidence identifies this capability as binding?
3. **Posture:** what strategic route was chosen, and which alternative was rejected?
4. **Intervention:** which JTBD, process, asset, or role will change the constraint?
5. **Authority:** who decides, who executes, who can stop the work, and who hears appeals?
6. **Resources:** cost, workforce, data, infrastructure, dependencies, and transition burden.
7. **Measures:** leading capability indicators, lagging outcomes, guardrails, and uncertainty.
8. **Review:** cadence, trigger conditions, sunset or rollback path, and independent verification.

## Prioritization rule

Do not rank work by numerical gap alone. Use an explicit rule such as:

`priority = expected outcome impact × urgency × tractability × evidence confidence ÷ cost and downside risk`

Publish the judgments behind each factor. A severe but weakly diagnosed gap should first generate a **measurement or diagnostic mission**, not a large deployment.

## Closing rule

The Map succeeds only when it preserves this chain:

> **Outcome deficit → evidenced capability constraint → deliberate posture → accountable mission → verified public result**

If the chain breaks, the work is activity without strategy—or measurement without consequence.
